About

I am an evolutionary biologist interested in how traits change across space and time. My research focuses on understanding how molecular variation—at the level of genes and genomes—translates into functional and phenotypic differences among populations and species.

Much of my work has centered on venomous snakes and the evolution of venom as a complex adaptive trait. I study venom variation using integrative genomic approaches, including population genomics, whole-genome sequencing, transcriptomics (RNA-seq of venom glands), and proteomic analyses of venom composition. By combining molecular data with functional assays, I aim to connect genetic and genomic differences to ecologically relevant variation in phenotype.

More broadly, I am interested in identifying general principles that link molecular evolution to trait evolution across diverse systems. While snakes and venom have provided a powerful model, I am widely interested in these questions across multiple species and traits—more recently applying similar genomic frameworks to migratory behavior and migration-associated fitness differences in songbirds.

Research

Pangenomes & structural variation

As a Postdoctoral Researcher in the Delmore Lab, I investigate how genomic architecture shapes the evolution of complex traits. After completing my Ph.D. examining how island biogeography predicts trait complexity and genetic diversity in rattlesnakes, I now apply similar evolutionary principles to seasonal migration in songbirds.

Specifically, I study how structural variants such as inversions and large-scale insertions/deletions contribute to variation in migratory behavior and influence hybrid fitness in the Swainson’s thrush. Migration is a complex, polygenic trait integrating behavior, physiology, and timing across annual cycles. By combining genomics and fitness data, I aim to understand how genomic architecture constrains or facilitates adaptation and speciation.

Venom evolution on islands

For my dissertation, I tested whether Island Biogeography Theory (IBT) can predict the evolution of functional trait complexity, particularly when that trait is shaped by multiple species interactions.

I used venom complexity in rattlesnakes as a model system. Venom is a polygenic trait shaped by predator–prey interactions and has been shown to increase with dietary breadth. Using IBT predictions about island area, isolation, and species diversity, we tested explicit hypotheses about how ecological context influences venom complexity across island populations of rattlesnakes in the Gulf of California.

Beyond phenotype, I also examine whether IBT predicts genomic diversity, including whether island characteristics shape individual heterozygosity and broader genomic architecture such as structural variation.
